According to the UN - Habitat Strategic Plan (2026-2029) and the Office of the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights (OHCHR), the global landscape of Housing represents a split-chord between legal mandate and basic necessity that must be met to live with dignity.  

Around 1.12 billion people  (an increase of 130 million since 2015 ) live in informal settlements , 300 million considered homeless. 
Approximately 1.6 billion people face “severe housing affordability constraints”, spending more than 30% of their income on rent/mortgages.

In light of these statistics, the following essay attempts to audit The 2017 Report on the “Financialization of Housing” , a landmark study presented to the  United Nations Human Rights Council by Leilani Farha, the Special Rapporteur on  adequate housing , that challenged  how the global economy treats residential property. 

### Right to Housing as a Fundamental Right
The first country to formally incorporate right to housing into a national constitution was Mexico(1917) A123 which began as a labour right that mandated that employers provide “comfortable and hygienic” housing for their workers, it set the global precedent for housing as a constitutional obligation of the state , followed by Germany (1919) after the Weimar Constitution (A155) that declared the distribution and use of land should be overseen by the state to ensure that “every German has a healthy dwelling.”

While these rights were often “programmatic” than enforceable in the early days including FD Roosevelt’s “Second Bill of Rights” (1944), contributing to US’s current status as a “rich country that doesn’t provide it” ,  only to take tangible  form after UN adopted the Universal Declaration of Human Rights in 1948 moving the concept from national experiments into a global “necessity”.

At present, over 170 countries  have ratified the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR) which includes the right to housing-only a smaller subset (84)provides it as a fundamental right for example A21 of Indian Constitution under “ Right to Life” . (Shantistar Builders v. Narayan Khimalal Totame, 1990).
Ironically, many developing nations have more robust constitutional language regarding housing than wealthier ones, often as a response to past colonial or systemic inequities such as South Africa, Brazil, Guyana & Haiti. 

Many “rich” nations the The Global North treats housing as a market commodity or statutory service having local “Right to Shelter” like New York City, “Right to Buy” United Kingdom, as Human Right in Canada’s in the same way as liberty and security as a result of Privatization and deregulation, making Finland the only  EU country where homelessness is falling, providing permanent housing first, without preconditions like sobriety or employment.

### Evolution of financialisation : Land as a “Capture Mechanism” to the financial engineering of 2005 

In The Wealth of Nations, Adam Smith defined land as a factor of production alongside Labour and Capital, which began to treat the right to occupy space as a cost of doing business or toll booths that every worker and capitalist  had to pass through. 

David Ricardo added variables of time and growth : He observed that as population increases, people move to cities (less productive land) and because the best land is limited, the owners of that prime land can charge more simply because the alternative is so much worse.

Ricardo proved that as the population grows and the economy improves, the “unearned increment” (the values of location) rises automatically. The house is no longer a “utility” (a place to live); but a “vehicle” to capture the future wealth of the community. 

That equation ran a different trajectory sold by Post WWII narrative that presented homeownership as a “reward”  intended to build  “Property -Owning Democracy” as coined by British Politician Noel Skelton but economically a stabilization tactic that shifted the worker’s status from  a “citizen with a right to shelter” to a “debtor with a duty to pay”. 
The Keynesian “Public Utility” note was played by the state (building mass housing), but the “Private Asset” (Collateral) note was played by the banks (financing the houses). 

Let's look at the structural flaws in this Asset-Based Welfare Model where “house” replaces the “state”:

The welfaristic  phase , popularized by Margarate Thatcher  “Wealth shouldn’t be just for the elite; everyone should own a piece of the country”  to  ensure health & productivity.

Phase 2 : Ricardo’s “Population Pressure” started showing its effect .The rise in house prices that created the wealth effect which would stimulate the economy. Houses were viewed as private safety nets. 

Phase 3 Neoliberal shift : Turning Homes into ATM : shrinking government pensions and social safety nets to  birth of Home Equity Lines of Credit (HELOCs)  Central banks like (Fed under the Greenspan) subsidized the interest rates , made borrowing cheap, causing people to bid up the price of land . Because the supply is fixed, the extra money provided by the subsidy didn’t make the house cheaper - it just allowed the seller to raise the price.

The subsidy intended for the worker was actually captured by the landowner . You could “withdraw” your home’s appreciation to pay for a child’s education, a car, or healthcare. 

Financial innovation such as the creation of Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS) : local banks stopped caring if you could pay back your loan because they sold the “ATM” debt to global investors. 
The “Ownership Mandate” policies like “Right to Buy”(UK) or subprime initiatives (US) pushed peo[ple with lower incomes  into the market, providing the “raw materials” for financial engineers to claim that thousands of risky “ATM withdrawals”  could be bundled into “safe” (AAA-rated) asset. By treating the home as a “Productive Asset” rather than a “Shelter Utility”,  they created a system that required house prices to outpace wages forever to remain solvent thus successfully offloading social responsibility onto the real estate market thereby giving rise to 2007-09 ‘s subprime epidemic. 

The global South followed a different , more chaotic trajectory than the North, the era of “Productive Neglect”. 
Post-colonial South  adopted a heavy Industry Bias following Nobel laureate A. Lewis’s dual sector model that emphasized development happens by moving “surplus labour” from traditional rural sector to modern industrial sector highlighting the belief that housing was consumption, not investment which led to structural neglect of urban planning and thus rise of informal settlements. 
Another economist’s  Harrod-Domar Model viewed housing as a drain on growth (1939/1946) requiring high capital - output ratio.  
Major developing countries such as India followed a similar model referred to as the Mahalanobis Strategy (2nd 5yr Plan) that focused on Steel, Power, Infrastructure and housing -the human element was deferred to the future. 
Nevertheless, population grew faster than the “Big Push” could trickle down and the 3rd world cities became sites of industrial growth surrounded by residential collapses (slums) until the De Soto Effect (1990s-2000s) intellectually bridged it to global financial integration transforming the crack into a frontier of capital in his The Mystery of Capital : giving the poor legal titles to their land unlocks the capital, allowing them to take loans, start business, and lift themselves out of poverty. 

### Tilting the Poor to Mapping for the Rich 

 What started as a plan to empower the residents became a tool for global assetization, the process of turning a physical space into a liquid financial instrument . It involves mapping the informal settlements using GPS and digital registries , making the land legible to international finance. 
 Tiled lands with astronomical “location value” were yield extracted by private equity funds or large developers by buying out the locals for high luxury rises bundled into Real Investment Trusts (REITs) and  traded  on stock exchanges in London or New York, marking successful assetization into a line of code in a global portfolio. 

### Convergence of the 1st or 3rd worlds 

By 2010’s the distinction between  the “ATM” West and the “Informal” 3rd world began to blur into a single global market. 
The 2008 crash proved the “ATM” was broken. The government did not go back to building social housing; instead they allowed Private Equity Firms like Blackstone to buy up thousands of foreclosed homes . Similarly “De Soto” logic was translated into massive land grabs to build a “Luxury Financial Asset: that often sit empty. 
It succeeded in making land “legal” but failed to keep it affordable, ensuring that the “unearned increment” ( the rising value of the city) was captured by global investors, marginalizing the  local residents to the peripherals. 

### Financialisation of Housing in the present context

The report highlights key effects such as the  rise of the hedged cities (e.g., London, New York, Hong Kong, Vancouver) as prime destinations for global capital. In these cities, housing prices skyrocket because investors use property to "hedge" against inflation or market volatility, effectively pricing out the middle and working classes, dehumanized housing  that leading to aggressive rent hikes, neglected maintenance, increased evictions to “reposition” buildings for higher-paying tenants.

A Ghost City (like Ordos New Town or parts of Zhengzhou in their early years) is the physical manifestation of housing as a "Gold Bar with Windows." In De Soto’s world, a title makes a home "live capital." In China, the "Ghost City" is a capital that is legally alive but socially dead. Thousands of apartments were bought as "stores of value." These cities prove Farha's point—housing is being used to park excess global and domestic capital, regardless of whether a single human being ever turns on a light switch.
Thus financialisation is a direct threat to the Right to Adequate Housing under international law

### Way Forward 
De-comodification, a direct way to stop “financialisation” of housing is to take it out of the market entirely.  Instead of the old "Welfare" model (state-owned blocks), this involves Community Land Trusts (CLTs) and Permanent Affordability Covenants which kills the Ricardian “unearned increment” and returns to being utility. 

Regulatory “Firewalls”  that force the disclosures of “Beneficial Ownership” making the landlord a visible legal entity is the first step toward accountability. 

Credit controls such as Limiting the “Loan-to-value” ratios for investment properties. If the banking system stops providing cheap leverage for "house flipping" or luxury vacancies, the bubble loses its primary fuel. 

“Housing  First” : The Finnish Strategy, The most successful modern way that prioritize strategic infrastructure investment with proven decrease in homelessness. 
This approach highlights the shift required to move away from the “property-owning Democracy” (which became a “Debt-owning Crisis”) toward a “Right-to-Dwelling” framework. 

### Blind Spots 
While the report is a brilliant diagnostic tool, critics and urban economists have identified several “low-resolution” areas and structural flaws in its logic:

Supply crisis : In many cities , even if you banned every hedge fund today, prices would remain high because of restrictive zoning, “NIMBism” and high construction costs. The report doesn't offer high-res solutions for building more. 

The Enforcement Gap : UN has no teeth . Calling housing a "Human Right" in a UN report doesn't change the law in Pune, London, or New York. The report lacks a roadmap for how to translate international "rights talk" into local "zoning and tax codes." 

Oversimplification of the “Institutional Investor” : Many "institutional investors" are actually Pension Funds (representing the savings of nurses, teachers, and firefighters). By attacking these investors, the report creates a paradox where the "workers"' retirement savings are the very thing driving the "workers"' housing costs up. The report doesn't solve this "circular" debt problem. 

As highlighted in reports many times, “welfare state” paradigm , today we cannot simply go back to the 
Mass state-build housing given the debt struck nature of modern states and lack of bureaucratic capacity to build at scale. We need to fully address how to leverage private capital in a way tat remains “humane” while providing adequate and sustainable shelter to millions that support SDG 11.
